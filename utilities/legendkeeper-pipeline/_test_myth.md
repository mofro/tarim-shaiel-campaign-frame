_The roads were here before the empire. That is the thing people forget._

They **forget** because empires are loud — they build walls and stamp seals and rename things in languages that coat their tongues with the taste of authority. And when they fall, the silence they leave behind is so deafening that your ears believe that the sound is of a world that has ended.

_**...But it hasn't.**_

The roads were **always here**. Under the paving stones and the marching boots and the bureaucratic waypoints, the roads were waiting. Patient as geography. Old as thirst.

**And the roads are busy now.**

**But**. There is a silence, too. Not the absence of sound — _the roads are never truly quiet._ This is something else. **A held breath**. A pause in the music that no one can quite identify because the song keeps playing. Something — _amiss_.

As if the road itself is waiting for someone.

_**And it has been waiting for a very long time.**_

## Moral

Roads endure what empires cannot: they serve need rather than power.

## Cultural Significance

Told across the oasis cities as both comfort and warning — the roads remember even when civilizations forget.
