# Player Character Archetypes: Five Modes of Heroism

## Overview

These archetypes are *thematic roles*, not classes. They describe the way a character relates to the world, their legendary past, and their place in Hero Heaven. A player can embody one of these archetypes while choosing any mechanical class or ability set.

The goal: **Offer flavor without prescription. Let players choose an archetype that resonates with how they see their hero.**

---

## 1. The Breaker (The Liberation Agent)

**Who they are:** The hero who shatters chains—literal or metaphorical. They break binding spells, free enslaved peoples, shatter oppressive systems. They remember themselves as liberators.

**Thematic tension:** Their liberation often had unintended consequences. Breaking one thing can destroy others. The freed Orcs they remember breaking loose? Those decades of chaos that followed? That's on them.

**In the world now:** They encounter descendants of people freed—and people harmed in the ensuing chaos. Some call them saviors. Some call them destroyers.

**Why this matters:** Perfect for exploring the question: "Was I right to act, even if the outcome was catastrophic?"

---

## 2. The Bridge (The Diplomat/Mediator)

**Who they are:** The hero who negotiated between warring factions, brokered uneasy peace, stood between worlds. They speak multiple languages, understand multiple cultures, and believe in finding common ground.

**Thematic tension:** Sometimes peace requires compromise that feels morally ambiguous. They may have negotiated with tyrants. They may have prioritized stability over justice.

**In the world now:** They see the current fragile peace and recognize their fingerprints on it—and wonder if it's genuine peace or just managed conflict.

**Why this matters:** Perfect for exploring: "Did I trade justice for stability? Was that the right call?"

---

## 3. The Seeker (The Truth-Teller/Scholar)

**Who they are:** The hero who sought forbidden knowledge, uncovered hidden truths, exposed corruption. They're investigators, scholars, sages—the ones who asked questions others wouldn't.

**Thematic tension:** Truth-seeking can be used to justify almost anything. They may have exposed secrets that led to mass suffering. Knowledge without wisdom is dangerous.

**In the world now:** They're naturally inclined to investigate their own expulsion. They *want* to know. But the truth might be worse than the myth.

**Why this matters:** Perfect for exploring: "Some truths shouldn't be spoken. Did I tell one?"

---

## 4. The Sacrificer (The Martyr/Idealist)

**Who they are:** The hero motivated by a pure ideal: justice, freedom, salvation, redemption. They sacrificed everything for their cause. They believe heroism requires personal cost.

**Thematic tension:** Idealism can justify cruelty "for the greater good." Their crusade may have crushed people who didn't deserve it.

**In the world now:** They cling to their ideals but encounter a world that's moved on—morally compromised, pragmatic, uncertain. Did their ideal actually matter?

**Why this matters:** Perfect for exploring: "Was my cause worth the cost? And whose cost was it, really?"

---

## 5. The Warrior (The Blade/Protector)

**Who they are:** The hero who fought directly—defeated enemies, protected the vulnerable, stood in the gap. They're soldiers, champions, guardians. They remember the battles clearly.

**Thematic tension:** Warriors follow orders. They may have been tools in someone else's larger plan. They killed people for causes they didn't fully understand.

**In the world now:** They meet the descendants of enemies they killed. People they thought were evil reveal themselves as more complex. Their victories feel hollow.

**Why this matters:** Perfect for exploring: "I was told who the enemy was. Was I told the truth?"

---

## 6. The Visionary (The Chosen One/Prophet)

**Who they are:** The hero touched by something larger—destiny, prophecy, divine will, or simply luck. They were *fated* to save the world. They remember it feeling inevitable.

**Thematic tension:** Destiny is a dangerous narrative. It can erase agency and complexity. Were they really chosen, or were they just convenient?

**In the world now:** The world has moved on without them. They're no longer necessary. Were they ever really special, or were they just the right person at the right time?

**Why this matters:** Perfect for exploring: "If I'm not the chosen one anymore, who am I?"

---

## How to Use These

**For Players:** Choose an archetype that resonates with how you see your character's legendary story. This shapes how they *remember* their past and what kinds of contradictions will challenge them most.

**For the Campaign:** These archetypes create natural friction. A Breaker and a Bridge might remember the same event very differently. A Warrior and a Seeker might question the other's conclusions. This diversity is the engine. Use it.

**Not Mutually Exclusive:** A character can blend two archetypes (e.g., "Bridge + Sacrificer" or "Seeker + Visionary"). Encourage creative combinations.

---

## Linking to the Larger Narrative

Each archetype invites specific questions about the Wizard, the fallen teammate, and Hero Heaven itself:

- **Breaker:** "Did I actually solve anything, or just delay the reckoning?"
- **Bridge:** "Can I trust this peace, or is it waiting to shatter?"
- **Seeker:** "What truth am I afraid to find?"
- **Sacrificer:** "Was my cause the right one, or did I just tell myself that?"
- **Warrior:** "If I wasn't fighting for the right side, what was I fighting for?"
- **Visionary:** "If destiny isn't real, what does that make me?"
