# The Malazan Magic System

## An overview of the Holds and Warrens

The Holds and the Warrens are planes of existence next to but separate from the
material world. For the sake of simplicity for new players, all of the Elder Warrens will
be referred to in this sourcebook as Holds because they all began as Holds (and
developed into / became known as Warrens over time), and are also not affected by
otataral in the way that the ‘normal’ Warrens are.

The Holds appear to have always existed, while the Warrens were created some
time after this (though still early in ancient history). Permanent gates can be found
within the Holds which lead to other Holds, and both the location of the Gate within
that Hold and which other Hold that specific Gate leads to are fixed. Opening these
permanent gates requires immense amounts of energy and skill, infinitely more than
most humans could ever manage. Portals from the material world to the Holds and
Warrens are known as Gates, and are temporary openings created by a mage.
For the most part, the Holds are normally only accessible to members of the race
that was created in them. 

The only known exceptions are the Malazan mage Bottle
(who could access the Holds instead of the Warrens) and mages born on the
continent of Lether (at least until a certain event in the books, though any GM
wishing to run a campaign on the continent of Lether should be familiar with this).
There are only an incredibly few humans that can draw on both Warrens and Holds
(such as Wickan warlocks), and it is unclear if any of the Ascendants can do so.
As noted in the section about spell selection during character creation, it is
important to be aware that humans can draw on multiple Warrens (up to 3 for most
mages, though the most powerful might be able to access more), while the Elder
races can only draw on their own Holds. This means that humans have far more
flexibility when choosing spells, though the Elder races are far more powerful due to
both their long lives and their very nature being linked to the Warren itself
(regardless of whether an individual member of the race was born in their Hold or in
the material world). For example, the Tiste Andii race were created in Kurald Galain
and so can only draw on that Hold.

## The Other Realms

There are a few other Holds and Warrens, but these do not seem to directly
provide any magical power, only serving as landscapes that can be travelled through
(and sometimes inhabited). These include: the Empty Hold, the Azath Hold, the Hold
of the Dead, the Imperial Warren, the Barghast Warren, the Refugium, and The
Abyss (though it is unclear whether the Abyss is a Warren or just a void outside of
the Warrens).

The Imperial Warren is a desolate landscape consisting entirely of bones, debris,
ruins, and ash. How it was created is not common knowledge, and it stood
abandoned for millennia until the Malazan Empire claimed it for their own. It is now
used by Malazan mages and members of the Claw (assassins) to travel faster than
in the material world and far more safely than in the other Warrens. You do not need
to currently be in service to the Malazan Empire to access it though you must have
been at some point in order to be taught how to access it. This means it is a risky
decision for deserters or traitors, as the only people they would meet while traveling
through the Imperial Warren will be Malazan loyalists.

There is also a plane of existence known as Chaos which seems to be a realm of
raw energy, and is hinted to be the substance that the various Warrens were created
from. It inhabits the space between Warrens (one of the reasons that it is incredibly
difficult to travel directly from one Warren to another). Drawing directly on Chaos would be extremely dangerous for even the most skilled mage, but this could happen
by accident if a mage tried to draw too heavily on their Warren (especially in an
emergency).

## Otataral and the nullification of magic

Otataral is an ore that nullifies all Warren magic within its area of effect by creating
a no-mana zone, dispelling any magic that has already been cast and any preventing
new spells from being attempted. It also dispels permanent and enchantment
Warren spells (in contrast to the Magic rulebook). Otataral does not affect Hold
magic at all.

Otataral is created when magic is used for incredibly powerful spells that end up
consuming all of the energy in the vicinity and leaving permanent dead zones. The
main source of this ore is on Otataral Island, where an ancient ritual went badly
wrong and created a huge amount of otataral, and the mine there is used as a prison
for mages and political prisoners (to prevent both escapes and rescues). It can also
be found at the few jade giant statues found across the material world, where the
otataral seems to develop as a scab or insulation around the statues. Mages
exposed to it will feel deeply unwell and will lose access to their warren(s) until they
have both left the area and cleaned themselves of all residual dust, so it is advisable
for party members to also thoroughly cleanse themselves.

The main ways that players will encounter otataral is either in its un-mined form
(which produces a lot of dust during the mining) or when the ore has been refined
and made into something (typically a weapon). The effects of otataral can be blocked
if the entirety of the otataral object is fully enclosed by a particularly dense object.
This is most frequently done with bronze or iron sheaths for weapons, or basalt
boxes for other types of items.

Wounds caused by otataral weapons cannot be healed by Denul magic at all,
making them the perfect choice for assassins whether or not their target is a mage.
Long-term exposure for mages does not permanently prevent mages accessing
their warrens, as they can regain access after some time away from the otataral, but
this is rarely of any help because the mage will almost certainly have lost their sanity
before they escape or are rescued from the mines.

The effects of long-term exposure on non-mages, either through continuous
contact with the dust or by carrying an otataral weapon for a long time, can also have
some side effects. The main positive side effect is that the wielder can also develop
the same magic-deadening properties, and an increased rate of healing is often seen
in conjunction with this (which helps to balance out the fact that healing magic will
now not work on them). There are some negative side effects though, such as
extreme overconfidence and a false belief in the character’s own invulnerability.

## The Unlimited Mana system


==“Convergence. Power ever draws other power.”==

There are a LOT of powerful characters native to the material world alone, let
alone the ones who were born elsewhere but now spend much of their time there
(such as Anomander Rake along with the rest of the Tiste Andii), and that’s even
before looking at the differences in power between the Ascendants and the ‘actual’
gods (and how individuals can change from one to the other). Many of them are
remarkably well-informed about events occurring in far parts of the world and how
that is likely to shape things to come, while some feel an almost magnetic pull
towards places where significant events are due to happen. These are known as
convergences.

These convergences are not directly caused by the amount of magical power
used in these events, though the events often correlate with significant amounts of
magic being used due to the vast majority of the characters described above having
access to some form of magic. Either way, lots of magic being used in a short space
of time is likely to draw attention from powers best avoided…

The boundaries of the Holds and Warrens themselves are also damaged, which
means that drawing on too much magic at once is also likely to end badly for the
caster. This is because the Warrens have been poisoned by the Warren of Chaos,
which is the Warren that exists in the gaps between the other Warrens. While the
Holds do not suffer from this in the same way, there are still cracks in their
boundaries, creating opportunities for beings to try find their way in. This damage
was caused during the **Chaining(s) of the Crippled God**.

Both of these factors combine to make using magic beyond the mage’s normal
abilities highly dangerous. However, there are times that the risk is worth it to the
players, and there could well be times when the GM chooses to allow more magic to
be used without requiring points to be added to the tally (such as during huge
battles, or when a convergence is currently happening elsewhere and so the player
characters could go unnoticed).

## The Power Tally

Spells cast under this system don’t cost FP. Rather, when a mage casts or
maintains a spell, record the listed cost in a running total – their power tally. All the
normal rules that modify energy cost (reduction for high skill, etc.) remain in force,
modifying the amount added to the tally instead. If the spell has a variable cost (e.g.,
an attack spell where the energy invested determines damage), then the caster can
vary the amount added to their tally in exactly the same way.

### Tally Recovery

A mage’s tally “heals” over time. The suggested standard recovery rate is 8 points
per day, with recovery occurring at sunrise. A mage can improve their recovery rate
by taking the Rapid Magical Recovery advantage, if the GM permits that. No other
traits reduce tally in any way.

### Thresholds

Every mage has a threshold score, defining safe limits on their magic. The
suggested standard threshold is 30. If a mage’s tally remains at or below their
threshold, then everything’s fine. If their tally exceeds their threshold, then bad things
can happen. They must make a Calamity Check – a roll on the Calamity Table
(below) – on any turn that they goes over their threshold for any reason. While they
are over their threshold, they must also make a Calamity Check on any turn that they
cast a spell (even one with zero cost) or add to their tally to maintain a spell.

### Optional Rule: Limited Tally Increase

To keep the destructive insanity under some control, the GM can set an absolute
upper limit on how much any mage can add to their tally with one spell. Five or 10
times the caster’s threshold should suffice.

### Calamity Checks

To make a Calamity Check, roll 3d and add 1 for every full 5 points by which tally
exceeds threshold after this casting. Calamities take effect immediately, but their
nature may not always be apparent to the mage (see the table for details).
Calamities don’t normally cause the spell to fail. However, if the Calamity Check total
is 29+, then the spell does fail unless the caster can make a Will roll at a penalty
equal to the bonus to the Calamity Check.
#### Calamity Table

This table is a suggestion. The GM can adjust and expand it to suit campaign
flavor and style, perhaps having hubristic magic summon hostile entities, affect the
weather in dramatic ways, and so on; see Appendix B (pp. 256-260) for inspiration.
For a dark and dangerous setting, the results might be more severe, and include
injury or the acquisition of physical disadvantages. In a comedy game, calamities
should be inconvenient and bizarre, but usually temporary. Even within the same
game world, the effects may vary by location (“the universe seems less forgiving in
the Dark Lands”) or by time. And the industrious GM could create a separate table
for each race or magical tradition.

Remember to consider calamities’ effects on the game world. For example, if a
fairly low roll reduces a mage’s Appearance, then there will be a lot of ugly mages.
Mage-friendly games might include spells that can repair calamity effects – with large
tally costs of their own, of course.

As with other magical disaster tables, anyone trying to trigger a calamity in the
hopes of seeing a specific result should find that this doesn’t work. Magic can seem
willful and perverse – and its disasters, doubly so. Suicide cults attempting to bring
down global curses through wild magic will instead find their magical abilities
destroyed and their dark gods no longer hearing their prayers. Provoking enemies
into overreaching themselves, on the other hand, can be an effective if dangerous
trick!

## Adjusting The Numbers

The numbers above should result in an overall mage power level roughly
equivalent to that under the standard rules – but different in style! Mages can’t cast
nearly as many smaller spells, but they can sometimes manage large magic; Area
spells, in particular, are more of an option in combat, and more-strategic magic
becomes possible. Most mages won’t risk a lot of big spells, but they can live
dangerously in emergencies. Trivial “utility” spells, on the other hand, may seem like
too much trouble. Professional mages-for-hire can’t offer as many simple services,
but might be more flamboyant. Careful energy-management strategies are no longer
an option, likely rendering some Quick and Dirty enchantments less viable and
making magic items rarer overall.

The basic numbers are all wide open to adjustment, however. Threshold and
recovery rate defaults are a campaign decision for the GM. A threshold of 50 and a
daily recovery rate of 1 would allow mages to cast the occasional really powerful
spell safely, but cripple them on a day-to-day basis. A threshold and recovery rate of
40 each would make for truly formidable mages, at least on the tactical level, and it
might be necessary to charge them a significant Unusual Background cost to keep
them balanced with other characters. At the other extreme, a threshold and recovery
rate of 5 each would create a distinctly low-magic world, but mages could still use
mighty magics in times of dire need – at huge personal risk.
