# Player Archetype Synthesis

⚠️ **WORKING DOCUMENT — NOT YET CANON**
This document synthesizes the established six archetypes with proposed expansions. Items marked [ESTABLISHED] are canonical (documented in CORE_CAMPAIGN_NARRATIVE.md). Items marked [PROPOSED] are under development and require Mo's approval before integration.

---

## PART 1: THE ARCHETYPE FRAMEWORK

### The Design Principle [ESTABLISHED]

Every hero archetype represents a **method** — a way of changing the world. Each method was genuinely heroic, genuinely necessary for liberating the enslaved peoples and defeating the Empire. Each method also carried unintended consequences the hero didn't see.

The archetype's **doubt** is not generic guilt. It is specific to their method, their tool, their way of being in the world. The hero doesn't question whether they're a good person. They question whether their *approach* — the thing that makes them *them* — was the right one.

### The Three-Layer Test

Every archetype must satisfy three requirements:

**1. The Method Must Have Been Necessary.**
The liberation could not have succeeded without this archetype's contribution. They weren't passengers. Their specific approach was essential to victory. If you remove them from the legendary past, the Empire doesn't fall.

**2. The Doubt Must Be Specific to the Method.**
The hero doesn't fear they did something wrong in general. They fear that their *specific way of doing things* caused harm that another approach might have avoided. The Breaker doesn't fear destruction abstractly — they fear that *their particular gift for breaking systems* freed the Wizard alongside the Orcs.

**3. The Tool Must Mirror the Doubt.**
Each hero's tool (weapon, artifact, capability) embodies their method AND their uncertainty. The tool Resists (won't perform as expected), Hungers (demands to be used in familiar ways), and Knows (carries fragments of the consequences). The hero's relationship with their tool IS their relationship with their own identity.

### The Narrative Function Test [PROPOSED]

Beyond the three-layer test, each archetype should serve a distinct **narrative function** in the campaign:

- **What story moments does this archetype uniquely create?**
- **What party dynamics does this archetype enable that wouldn't exist otherwise?**
- **What aspect of the campaign's themes does this archetype carry?**

If two archetypes create substantially similar story moments, one of them is redundant — or they need to be differentiated more sharply.

## PART 2: THE ESTABLISHED SIX

### The Warrior [ESTABLISHED]

**Method:** Crushes opposition, provides protection through overwhelming force, ends threats with decisive action.

**Legendary Role:** Defeated the Empire's armies. The military backbone of the liberation. Without the Warrior's force, the Empire's physical power could not have been broken.

**Fear:** *"Did my overwhelming force scatter the Wizard rather than destroy him? Did my focus on crushing enemies prevent me from seeing the real threat?"*

**Deeper Question:** *"Is my strength my greatest asset, or is it a blindness?"*

**Tool:** Weapon of force — currently crude, heavy, bound. Resists by feeling weakened. Hungers for old patterns of decisive action. Knows every battle fought and the one enemy that escaped.

**Narrative Function:** Physical anchor. Protection instinct. The force-vs-understanding axis. Creates moments where the obvious solution (hit it harder) must be weighed against subtler approaches.

**Unique Party Dynamic:** The one the party looks to when danger is physical. The one who must learn that not every problem is a nail.

**Campaign Theme Carried:** Strength vs. wisdom. Protection vs. control.

---

### The Seeker [ESTABLISHED]

**Method:** Uncovers knowledge, questions assumptions, digs into forbidden truths, reveals hidden things.

**Legendary Role:** Found the truths that made liberation possible — the Wizard's binding spells, the Empire's weaknesses, the paths to victory that no one else could see. Without the Seeker's discoveries, the heroes would have been fighting blind.

**Fear:** *"What if the truths I found became weapons in the Wizard's hands? What if my refusal to accept easy answers opened doors that should have stayed closed?"*

**Deeper Question:** *"Who is answering when I look? Am I finding truth, or am I being shown what someone else wants me to see?"*

**Tool:** Tool of clarity — tome, compass, astrolabe. Resists by showing different truths than expected. Hungers to go deeper, reveal bigger secrets. Knows what it revealed and whether that knowledge was weaponized.

**Narrative Function:** Investigative spine. The one who drives discovery, uncovers the Wizard's survival, forces "can we trust what we know?" The campaign cannot progress without someone whose method is finding things out.

**Unique Party Dynamic:** The one who asks "but why?" when everyone else is ready to act. The one whose curiosity creates both opportunities and dangers.

**Campaign Theme Carried:** Knowledge vs. deception. The cost of knowing.

---

### The Breaker [ESTABLISHED]

**Method:** Shatters systems, breaks chains, destroys oppressive structures.

**Legendary Role:** Broke the Empire's binding spells on the Orcs. Shattered the institutional structures that maintained subjugation. Without the Breaker, the enslaved peoples would still be enslaved.

**Fear:** *"Did breaking the Empire also break the containment holding the Wizard? Every system I shattered may have been oppressive, but was something in that oppression also a restraint?"*

**Deeper Question:** *"Is my gift for breaking the gift of liberation, or am I fundamentally destructive?"*

**Tool:** Hammer — crude, heavy, powerful. Resists by growing heavier when aimed at certain targets. Hungers for destruction. Knows every structure that fell and the chaos that followed.

**Narrative Function:** Moral fulcrum. Consequence-bearer. Forces the party to confront whether liberation was worth the cost. Creates moments where destruction is available but its consequences are uncertain.

**Unique Party Dynamic:** The most dangerous member of the party — not because they're evil, but because their instinct is to *break things*, and some things shouldn't be broken. Natural foil to any archetype that builds or preserves.

**Campaign Theme Carried:** Liberation vs. destruction. Unintended consequences. Freedom's cost.

---

### The Visionary [ESTABLISHED]

**Method:** Sees the true path forward, provides guidance, illuminates the future.

**Legendary Role:** Saw the path to liberation — the sequence of events, the strategic vision, the future worth fighting for. Without the Visionary's guidance, the heroes would have been reactive rather than strategic.

**Fear:** *"Was I seeing truth, or seeing what I wanted to see? Was someone else guiding my sight?"*

**Deeper Question:** *"Can I trust my own sight? What if my clarity about the future has been compromised from the start?"*

**Tool:** Crystal/mirror/oracle device — showing flashes but unreliably. Resists by showing contradictory visions. Hungers to pull the hero further along paths that may not be their own. Knows every path shown and whether those paths were genuine.

**Narrative Function:** Direction and paranoia. The one who *should* know where to go — but can't trust their own guidance. Creates tension about whether the party is on the right path or being led into a trap.

**Unique Party Dynamic:** The party looks to them for direction. The weight of that expectation — and the Visionary's inability to be certain — creates constant low-level tension.

**Campaign Theme Carried:** Foresight vs. manipulation. Trust in guidance. The burden of leadership through vision.

---

### The Bridge (Diplomat) [ESTABLISHED]

**Method:** Unites disparate peoples, negotiates compromise, builds alliances, brings opposing forces together.

**Legendary Role:** United the diverse peoples of the resistance — Orcs, Dwarves, Elves, Humans — into a coalition capable of defeating the Empire. Without the Bridge, the liberation would have been fragmented, uncoordinated, and likely defeated piecemeal.

**Fear:** *"Did my pragmatism — my willingness to work within existing systems — inadvertently strengthen them? Did I build a network that became the Wizard's network?"*

**Deeper Question:** *"Is my gift for unity genuine wisdom, or is it unconscious control?"*

**Tool:** Diplomatic seal/token — implying authority the hero doesn't remember having. Resists by failing to persuade certain people. Hungers to gather, build consensus, influence. Knows every alliance brokered and whether any became the Wizard's tools.

**Narrative Function:** Political complexity. Alliance-building. The one who navigates the Silk Road's competing factions and cultural tensions. Creates moments where compromise is necessary but its costs are hidden.

**Unique Party Dynamic:** The social engine. The one who talks when others fight. But also the one whose influence might be manipulation they can't see.

**Campaign Theme Carried:** Unity vs. control. Compromise vs. complicity. Trust across difference.

**Note:** CORE_CAMPAIGN_NARRATIVE.md contains both "Bridge" and "Diplomat" as separate entries with substantially similar methods/doubts. Bridge emphasizes unity/alliance; Diplomat emphasizes negotiation/systems. These can be presented as two expressions of the same archetype or as distinct archetypes if a table wants both. See CORE_CAMPAIGN_NARRATIVE.md for the Diplomat variant.

---

### The Sacrificer [ESTABLISHED]

**Method:** Gives of themselves, channels will toward others' needs, accepts sacrifice as the price of justice.

**Legendary Role:** Paid the personal costs that made victory possible. When the price of liberation was suffering, the Sacrificer absorbed it. Their willingness to give everything — and the companion who fell beside them — became the rallying cry that won the war.

**Fear:** *"Was the defining sacrifice genuine, or was it orchestrated? Is the ghost of my fallen companion trying to help me, or is it being used against me?"*

**Deeper Question:** *"Is my willingness to sacrifice my greatest strength, or is it a vulnerability that others exploit?"*

**Tool:** Ritual implement — dormant, warm to the touch, waiting. Resists by preventing the old patterns of self-sacrifice. Hungers to channel that giving energy again. Knows the history of what was sacrificed and whether it served the intended purpose.

**Narrative Function:** Emotional heart. Cost and consequence made personal. The one who asks "what is this worth?" when others focus on tactics. Creates moments where the party must decide what they're willing to lose.

**Unique Party Dynamic:** The conscience. The one who makes sacrifice visible and personal. But also the one whose compulsive giving can become a burden on others.

**Campaign Theme Carried:** Cost vs. value. Sacrifice vs. self-destruction. Receiving as strength.

**Note:** CORE_CAMPAIGN_NARRATIVE.md also contains "The Martyr" as a variant emphasizing the defining sacrifice and the fallen companion. These may be the same archetype (Martyr as legendary identity, Sacrificer as present-day pattern) or distinct. See CORE_CAMPAIGN_NARRATIVE.md for the Martyr variant.

## PART 3: PROPOSED EXPANSION ARCHETYPES

### Design Methodology

The established six cover methods of: **Destroying, Fighting, Discovering, Seeing, Uniting, and Giving.** These are all *active* methods — they DO things to the world.

The proposed expansions explore methods that are more *responsive* or *structural*: Preserving, Mending, Witnessing, Creating, and Subverting.

Each proposed archetype includes the three-layer test and the narrative function test.

---

### The Sentinel (Watcher/Witness) [PROPOSED] — Recommended: HIGH

**Method:** Stood watch. Bore witness. Observed, recorded, ensured accountability. Not foresight (Visionary) but present-tense *observation* — seeing what IS, not what will be.

**Legendary Role:** The moral conscience of the liberation. The record-keeper who ensured the heroes remained accountable. The one who watched every decision, every consequence, every compromise — and documented truthfully. Without the Sentinel, the heroes would have lost track of their own principles amid the chaos of war.

**Fear:** *"I watched everything. I saw the Wizard retreat rather than fall. I recorded the gaps — the things that didn't add up, the loose threads. I KNEW something was incomplete. And I stayed silent because the record showed victory, and questioning victory felt like treason."*

**Deeper Question:** *"Is witnessing enough? At what point does the observer have a duty to intervene? When does faithful recording become complicity through silence?"*

**Tool:** A recording device — journal, memory stone, inscribed tablet. Resists by refusing to record certain things (or recording things the Sentinel didn't intend). Hungers to observe, to watch, to document. Knows everything that was witnessed — including the things the Sentinel chose not to report.

**Narrative Function:** Fills a gap nothing else covers — the duty to speak vs. the discipline of observation. The Sentinel's doubt ("I knew and said nothing") is the most direct connection to the heroes' collective failure. Every other archetype failed because they didn't SEE the Wizard's survival. The Sentinel failed because they DID see — or saw the signs — and stayed silent.

**Unique Party Dynamic:** The one who notices things others miss. Not through investigation (Seeker) or foresight (Visionary) but through *paying attention to what's right in front of everyone*. The one who says "I saw that" when others are looking away. But also the one who might stay silent too long.

**Campaign Theme Carried:** Observation vs. action. The duty to speak. Complicity through silence.

**Foil Relationships:**
- **Visionary** sees the future; **Sentinel** sees the present. Different failure modes of perception.
- **Seeker** actively hunts for hidden truths; **Sentinel** passively absorbs visible ones. Different methods of knowing.
- **Bridge** shapes events through influence; **Sentinel** records events without shaping them. Different relationships with power.

**Awakening Sketch:** Wakes with perfect clarity about surroundings — sees everything, understands context immediately. But cannot ACT. Paralyzed by the habit of observation. Must break the pattern of watching and DO something. Perhaps: observes a crime or injustice unfolding and must choose between documenting it faithfully and stopping it. The tool records everything they see — including their own hesitation.

**Alternate Awakening Concept (from Visionary swap):** Inherit the Visionary's "mid-crisis, must act NOW" structural position. The Sentinel wakes INTO an unfolding disaster — and unlike the Visionary (whose gift fails), the Sentinel's gift works *perfectly*. They see everything: who's in danger, what's about to collapse, where the threat is coming from. Perfect observation. And it's not enough. The crisis demands they stop watching and DO. The irony isn't "I can't use my gift" — it's "my gift is working and it's STILL not enough." This directly echoes the legendary failure (saw the signs, chose silence) and forces the player to confront the core doubt in the first minutes of play. Potentially the strongest metaphoric match of any awakening scenario.

*Status: Concept only. Not committed. Parking for future development.*

**Silk Road Resonance:** Chroniclers at caravanserais. The tradition of the *tarikh* (historical chronicle) in Islamic scholarship. Court historians. The tension between recording truth and serving power.

**Three-Layer Test:**
1. ✅ Method was necessary — the liberation needed accountability and record-keeping to maintain legitimacy.
2. ✅ Doubt is specific — the fear isn't about action taken, but about silence kept. Unique among all archetypes.
3. ✅ Tool mirrors doubt — the recording device that knows what was witnessed, including what was suppressed.

**Assessment:** Strongest proposed addition. Fills a genuine gap. The doubt is devastating and directly connects to the heroes' collective failure. The Sentinel is the only archetype whose failure was *knowing and not speaking* rather than *acting and not seeing*.

---

### The Keeper (Guardian/Preserver) [PROPOSED] — Recommended: HIGH

**Method:** Preserved what others wanted to destroy. Protected institutions, knowledge, culture, sacred sites — not through fighting (Warrior) but through *maintaining*.

**Legendary Role:** While the Breaker destroyed the Empire's oppressive structures, the Keeper preserved what was worth keeping — knowledge systems, cultural heritage, sacred traditions that predated the Empire and would outlast it. Without the Keeper, liberation would have been a bonfire consuming the good along with the bad.

**Fear:** *"What I preserved — was it worth preserving? Did I protect the old systems because they were valuable, or because I was afraid of change? Did my preservation of imperial infrastructure give the Wizard shelter? Are the things I saved the things that should have burned?"*

**Deeper Question:** *"Is preservation wisdom or cowardice? When do you stop protecting the past and let the future happen?"*

**Tool:** A key, a seal, a protective ward — something that locks/unlocks, shields/reveals. Resists by locking things the Keeper wants open (or unlocking things that should stay sealed). Hungers to protect, shield, maintain. Knows everything preserved — and whether any of it sheltered the Wizard.

**Narrative Function:** The natural foil to the Breaker. One destroys; the other preserves. Together they embody the central tension of the liberation: what do you tear down, and what do you protect? Creates moments where the party must decide whether an old institution is worth saving or needs to be dismantled.

**Unique Party Dynamic:** The conservative voice — *conserving*, not politically conservative. The one who says "wait, before we destroy this, are we sure it doesn't serve a purpose?" Values continuity, tradition, institutional memory. In tension with the Breaker but not in opposition — two sides of the same coin.

**Campaign Theme Carried:** Preservation vs. stagnation. The value of the past. What's worth keeping.

**Foil Relationships:**
- **Breaker ↔ Keeper** — Strongest foil pair in the expanded roster. One destroys systems; the other preserves them. Their arguments about discovered Imperial ruins will be the campaign's most productive debates.
- **Seeker** uncovers hidden knowledge; **Keeper** protects existing knowledge. Different relationships with truth.
- **Sacrificer** gives things away; **Keeper** holds things close. Different relationships with value.

**Awakening Sketch:** Wakes surrounded by something fragile — a crumbling archive, cultural artifacts being scavenged for materials, a sacred site being dismantled. Must choose between saving what remains and letting it go. The tool (key/ward) is protecting something the Keeper might not want protected.

**Silk Road Resonance:** The Dunhuang cave temples. Preservation of texts through centuries of upheaval. Caravanserais as cultural preservation sites. Dwarven vaults. Elven sacred sites predating the Empire. Very strong thematic fit with established world-building.

**Three-Layer Test:**
1. ✅ Method was necessary — someone had to preserve cultural heritage during the chaos of liberation.
2. ✅ Doubt is specific — the fear that preservation sheltered the enemy. The inverse of the Breaker's doubt.
3. ✅ Tool mirrors doubt — the key/ward that protects things, including things that maybe shouldn't be protected.

**Assessment:** Second strongest proposed addition. The Breaker/Keeper foil is the most narratively productive pairing among the expansions. The Silk Road setting gives this archetype rich, grounded material.

---

### The Trickster (Deceiver/Subverter) [PROPOSED] — Recommended: MEDIUM-HIGH (with caution)

**Method:** Worked from within. Used deception, misdirection, infiltration. The spy, the saboteur, the one who wore the enemy's face and unmade them from the inside.

**Legendary Role:** Infiltrated the Empire's networks. Sabotaged from within. Turned the enemy's own systems against them. Without the Trickster's subversion, the Empire's intelligence apparatus would have detected and crushed the liberation before it succeeded.

**Fear:** *"I lied so well that I'm not sure I remember which face is real. I infiltrated the Empire's networks so deeply that I became part of them. The Wizard was a master of deception — did he learn it from watching me? Did I teach him how to hide?"*

**Deeper Question:** *"Can you use deception for good without losing yourself? When you spend years wearing masks, do you forget what's underneath?"*

**Tool:** A mask, a cloak, a mirror that shows different faces. Resists by refusing to show the true face (or showing faces the Trickster doesn't recognize). Hungers for deception, disguise, the thrill of becoming someone else. Knows every identity worn — and whether any were more real than the "original."

**Narrative Function:** The most morally complex archetype. Creates immediate party tension: can you trust the one whose method was deception? Forces the party to confront whether the ends justify the means.

**Unique Party Dynamic:** The one no one fully trusts — including themselves. Can solve problems no one else can (infiltration, misdirection) but at a cost to party cohesion. The Bridge builds trust; the Trickster exploits it. Not enemies, but never comfortable.

**Campaign Theme Carried:** Truth vs. deception. Identity vs. performance. The cost of wearing masks.

**Foil Relationships:**
- **Bridge** builds trust through honesty; **Trickster** achieves goals through deception. Both social operators with opposite methods.
- **Sentinel** observes truth; **Trickster** creates falsehood. Different relationships with what's real.
- **Seeker** uncovers hidden truths; **Trickster** creates hidden lies. Complementary but tension-generating.

**Awakening Sketch:** Wakes not knowing who they are — genuinely. Not just expulsion amnesia but deeper identity crisis. Multiple potential selves compete. Perhaps: wakes in a crowd, realizes people are reacting to them as someone specific, must decide whether to play the role or break it. The tool keeps showing different faces — which one is real?

**Silk Road Resonance:** Trade networks as intelligence networks. Spies, informants, competing merchant factions. The flow of information alongside goods. Dwarven selective access. Post-imperial political suspicion.

**Three-Layer Test:**
1. ✅ Method was necessary — the Empire's intelligence apparatus required subversion from within.
2. ✅ Doubt is specific — the fear of losing the true self to the masks. Unique existential crisis.
3. ✅ Tool mirrors doubt — the mask/cloak showing faces the hero doesn't recognize.

**Assessment:** The most narratively dangerous and the most interesting. Requires a player who can handle moral complexity without derailing. Creates productive tension with nearly every other archetype. Best introduced after Session 0, once the core party has bonded.

**⚠️ Design Caution:** Risks becoming "the party member no one trusts" in an unfun way. Requires a player who understands that deception should create dramatic irony (audience knows what the Trickster does) not player frustration (no one knows). GM management essential.

---

### The Crafter (Builder/Maker) [PROPOSED] — Recommended: MEDIUM

**Method:** Built the tools, infrastructure, weapons, and systems that made liberation possible. Not destruction (Breaker) but *creation*.

**Legendary Role:** Forged the weapons that armed the resistance. Built infrastructure sustaining a guerrilla campaign across thousands of miles. Engineered siege equipment that broke the Empire's fortifications. Without the Crafter, the heroes would have had conviction without capability.

**Fear:** *"The weapons I forged — who else used them after the war? The systems I built — did the Wizard repurpose them? Every tool I created was meant for liberation, but tools don't care about intent."*

**Deeper Question:** *"Can a maker control what their creations become? Is the builder responsible for what's built with their foundations?"*

**Tool:** A crafting implement — hammer for shaping (distinct from Breaker's hammer for smashing), chisel, forge-tongs, a set of plans. Resists by refusing to create certain things (or creating things unintended). Hungers to build, improve, perfect. Knows everything built — and what those creations became after the war.

**Narrative Function:** The practical problem-solver. Creates moments where the party needs something MADE. Also creates moments where something the Crafter built (in the legendary past) is now being used against them.

**Unique Party Dynamic:** The one who says "I can build that." The practical counterpoint to the Seeker's theory and the Visionary's strategy. But also the one whose creations have unintended lives of their own.

**Campaign Theme Carried:** Creation vs. consequence. The responsibility of makers. Tools that outlive intent.

**Foil Relationships:**
- **Breaker** destroys; **Crafter** builds. Both deal with the physical world. Not direct opposition (the Keeper is the Breaker's true opposite) but complementary approaches with mirrored consequences.
- **Seeker** discovers knowledge; **Crafter** applies it. Theory vs. practice.

**Awakening Sketch:** Wakes surrounded by broken things. The instinct to fix and build is immediate. Perhaps: finds themselves amid mechanical wreckage with people stranded, must improvise solutions from available materials. The tool suggests designs the Crafter doesn't recognize — blueprints for things their hands remember building but their mind doesn't.

**Silk Road Resonance:** Dwarven trade-strongholds and engineering. Irrigation systems. Caravanserai architecture. Bridge-building across mountain passes. The physical infrastructure of the trade routes.

**Three-Layer Test:**
1. ✅ Method was necessary — the resistance needed weapons, infrastructure, and engineering.
2. ✅ Doubt is specific — the fear that creations were repurposed. Different from Breaker's fear (destruction's consequences vs. creation's consequences).
3. ✅ Tool mirrors doubt — the crafting implement that remembers what it built and what those things became.

**Assessment:** Solid and thematic. Functionally useful in a party. The doubt is clean. Overlaps with the Breaker in dealing with physical-world consequences. Best differentiated by emphasizing the Crafter's creative optimism vs. the Breaker's destructive guilt.

**⚠️ Design Caution:** The Crafter's tool must be clearly distinct from the Breaker's hammer. Different purpose (shaping vs. smashing), different feel, different voice. The Breaker's hammer hungers to destroy; the Crafter's implement hungers to create.

---

### The Healer (Mender/Restorer) [PROPOSED] — Recommended: LOW-MEDIUM

**Method:** Repaired what was broken. Not just bodies — relationships, communities, land scarred by war.

**Legendary Role:** Tended the wounded. Restored communities shattered by war. Healed land where magic had scarred it. Without the Healer, the liberation would have been a victory standing on corpses — survivors would have had freedom but not the capacity to use it.

**Fear:** *"Did I heal the symptoms while the disease grew? Every community I restored — was I giving people a false sense of recovery while the real threat went unaddressed? Did comfort become the enemy of vigilance?"*

**Deeper Question:** *"Is healing sometimes a way of avoiding the harder truth that some things shouldn't be repaired — they should be rebuilt differently?"*

**Tool:** A mending implement — needle and thread, salve-pot, restorative staff. Resists by refusing to heal certain wounds (or healing things better left broken). Hungers to mend, restore, make whole. Knows everything healed — and whether that healing masked deeper problems.

**Narrative Function:** The caretaker. Creates moments where the party must decide whether to heal a wound or let it remain as a reminder. The Healer discovers that restorative work (in the legendary past) made communities vulnerable through comfort.

**Unique Party Dynamic:** The one everyone turns to when hurt. But also the one whose instinct to fix things can prevent necessary confrontation with painful truths.

**Campaign Theme Carried:** Recovery vs. complacency. The cost of comfort. Healing as avoidance.

**Foil Relationships:**
- **Sacrificer** gives of themselves; **Healer** gives to others. Both givers with different costs (personal depletion vs. systemic complacency).
- **Breaker** destroys what's broken; **Healer** repairs what's broken. Different responses to damage.
- **Seeker** disrupts comfort with uncomfortable truths; **Healer** restores comfort. Natural tension.

**Awakening Sketch:** Wakes to someone in need. The instinct to help is overwhelming. Finds an injured traveler, tends to them, discovers the wound is a symptom of a larger problem requiring confrontation rather than restoration.

**Three-Layer Test:**
1. ✅ Method was necessary — the liberation produced massive casualties and damaged communities.
2. ✅ Doubt is specific — the fear that healing created complacency. Different from Sacrificer's fear.
3. ✅ Tool mirrors doubt — the mending implement that healed things that maybe needed to stay broken.

**Assessment:** Most conventional fantasy archetype. Functional and thematic but overlaps most with the Sacrificer. Include if the table specifically wants a healer AND doesn't have a Sacrificer, or if the distinction (personal cost vs. systemic cost) justifies both.

**⚠️ Design Caution:** Risk of being "the cleric." Must be framed as narrative archetype, not mechanical role. The crisis is about the *consequences* of healing, not the *act* of healing.

## PART 4: ARCHETYPE RELATIONSHIPS — THE WEB OF FOILS

### Primary Foil Pairs

The richest campaign moments come from archetypes that are *foils* — not enemies, but mirrors reflecting different aspects of the same dilemma.

**Breaker ↔ Keeper** [STRONGEST FOIL]
One destroys systems; the other preserves them. The liberation's central tension made personal. Their debates about discovered Imperial ruins will drive some of the campaign's best scenes.

**Seeker ↔ Trickster**
One uncovers hidden truths; the other creates hidden lies. Both deal in information — one to reveal, the other to manipulate. Need each other. Can't fully trust each other.

**Warrior ↔ Sacrificer**
Both protect others through opposite methods — strength vs. self-depletion. The Warrior fears strength as blindness; the Sacrificer fears giving as vulnerability. Together they define what protection actually costs.

**Visionary ↔ Sentinel**
One sees the future; the other sees the present. Both perceivers with different failure modes. The Visionary fears manipulated sight; the Sentinel fears complicit silence. Together: what good is seeing if you don't act?

**Bridge ↔ Trickster**
Both social operators — one builds trust through honest influence, the other achieves goals through deception. Will fundamentally disagree about how to handle nearly every social situation.

### Secondary Tensions

- **Crafter ↔ Breaker:** One builds, one destroys. Different expressions of the same impulse to shape physical reality.
- **Healer ↔ Seeker:** One restores comfort, the other disrupts it with uncomfortable truths.
- **Keeper ↔ Visionary:** One protects the past, the other guides toward the future. Which direction should the party face?
- **Sentinel ↔ Sacrificer:** One watches cost accumulate without speaking; the other pays cost silently without being asked. Both enablers of suffering through inaction, from different angles.
- **Crafter ↔ Keeper:** One creates new things; the other preserves old ones. Both value what endures but disagree about whether the future should look like the past.

## PART 5: RECOMMENDATIONS

### For Player Choice Presentation

Players should see:
- **The Method** (what this hero did during the liberation)
- **The Core Question** (what they now doubt — framed as question, not spoiler)
- **The Tool** (what object connects them to their legendary past)
- **The Tone** (what kind of stories this archetype creates)

Specific fears, the Wizard connection, and cosmological architecture remain GM knowledge.

### Expansion Priority

If expanding beyond six:

1. **Sentinel** — fills the most distinctive gap. The only archetype whose failure was *knowing and not speaking*.
2. **Keeper** — creates the strongest new foil pair (with Breaker). Rich Silk Road material.
3. **Trickster** — most interesting but most dangerous. Requires the right player.
4. **Crafter** — solid, practical, thematically grounded. Good for mechanically-minded players.
5. **Healer** — only if no Sacrificer, or if the table specifically wants the distinction.

### Ranked Choice — Full Roster (All 11 Archetypes)

The following ranking evaluates all eleven archetypes (six established + five proposed) against three criteria: narrative function gap-filling, quality of the archetype-specific doubt, and scene-generating potential within the Silk Road setting. The three essential archetypes provide a physical anchor, an investigative engine, and moral weight. What's missing from that trio is a social engine, a perception axis, and an emotional register.

**Tier 1 — The Engine (Essential, non-negotiable)**

| Rank | Archetype | Status | Rationale |
|------|-----------|--------|-----------|
| 1 | **Warrior** | ESTABLISHED | Physical anchor. Protection instinct. Force-vs-understanding axis. |
| 2 | **Seeker** | ESTABLISHED | Investigative spine. Discovery engine. The campaign cannot progress without someone whose method is finding things out. |
| 3 | **Breaker** | ESTABLISHED | Moral fulcrum. Consequence-bearer. Carries the campaign's most uncomfortable question. |

**Tier 2 — The Heart (Strongly recommended)**

| Rank | Archetype | Status | Rationale |
|------|-----------|--------|-----------|
| 4 | **Sentinel** | PROPOSED | Fills the perception gap AND carries the most devastating doubt. The only archetype whose failure was passive — knowing and not speaking. More directly implicated in the heroes' collective failure than any other. If only one archetype is added to the core three, this is the one. |
| 5 | **Bridge** | ESTABLISHED | Fills the social engine gap. The Silk Road campaign needs someone who talks, negotiates, builds alliances. Without the Bridge, the setting's political richness goes underused. |

**Tier 3 — The Depth (Excellent additions)**

| Rank | Archetype | Status | Rationale |
|------|-----------|--------|-----------|
| 6 | **Keeper** | PROPOSED | Completes the Breaker foil. Every scene involving Imperial relics, cultural artifacts, Dwarven vaults, Elven sacred sites doubles in value with both Breaker and Keeper present. Scene-generating power the Sacrificer doesn't provide. |
| 7 | **Sacrificer** | ESTABLISHED | The emotional register. The convergence integration is structurally elegant. "Receiving as strength" is genuinely moving. But the doubt is the most personal and least plot-driving. |

**Tier 4 — The Spice (Right player, right table)**

| Rank | Archetype | Status | Rationale |
|------|-----------|--------|-----------|
| 8 | **Trickster** | PROPOSED | Most interesting on paper. Identity crisis is existentially compelling. But high-risk — wrong player poisons party trust. Best as Model C late introduction after core party has bonded. |
| 9 | **Visionary** | ESTABLISHED | The hardest cut from the original six. The Sentinel does the perception job better (present-tense observation vs. unreliable future-sight). The Seeker does the guidance job adequately (investigation vs. vision). The Visionary's unique contribution — paranoia about manipulated direction — is powerful seasoning but not load-bearing. Most overlap with the Seeker in party function. |
| 10 | **Crafter** | PROPOSED | Solid and practical. But the Breaker already covers physical consequences, the Keeper covers relationship with what endures. The Crafter's middle position, while valid, isn't as distinctive. |
| 11 | **Healer** | PROPOSED | Only if no Sacrificer. The overlap with the Sacrificer is too significant otherwise. |

### Recommended "Best Six" Roster

If building a party of six from the full eleven:

| Slot | Archetype | Replaces | Function |
|------|-----------|----------|----------|
| 1 | **Warrior** | — | Physical anchor |
| 2 | **Seeker** | — | Investigative spine |
| 3 | **Breaker** | — | Moral fulcrum |
| 4 | **Sentinel** | Visionary | Perception axis (present-tense, more devastating doubt) |
| 5 | **Bridge** | — | Social engine |
| 6 | **Keeper** | Sacrificer | Breaker's foil; scene-generating power |

The Sacrificer moves to "woven into convergence if a seventh player appears." The Visionary becomes a Model A wanderer or Model C late addition (see Party_Composition_Design_Notes.md). The Trickster is the wildcard for a player who specifically wants moral complexity.

**Hardest cut:** Sacrificer over Keeper for slot 6. The Sacrificer's emotional register is beautiful, but the Keeper provides structural scene-generation — every Imperial relic, cultural artifact, or sacred site becomes a Breaker/Keeper debate.

**Most controversial take:** Sentinel over Visionary. The Visionary is one of the original six, but "I could see the future and now I can't" is less narratively devastating than "I could see the present and chose not to speak." The Sentinel's doubt implicates them in the heroes' failure more directly than any other archetype.

### Minimum Viable Party Reminder

Three archetypes tell the full story: **Warrior** (force), **Seeker** (knowledge), **Breaker** (consequence). Everything else enriches. The graduated awakening model (see Session_0_Awakening_Design_Notes.md) supports 3-8+ players without structural change.
