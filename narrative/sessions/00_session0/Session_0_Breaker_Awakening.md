# Session 0: Breaker Awakening

**Archetype:** Breaker  
**Core Doubt:** "Can I be present without destroying everything I touch?"  
**Tool:** Hammer (crude, heavy, wedged as structural support)  
**Setting:** Collapsed inn/tavern after earthquake  
**Crisis:** Can escape if hammer is abandoned; taking it risks everyone
**Duration:** 40-45 minutes total play time (estimated)
**Tone:** Sensory confinement → geometric discovery → ethical weight → identity choice

---

## Campaign Context

This awakening serves the larger Session 0 architecture:
- **Individual crisis** specific to Breaker archetype (identity vs. survival, power vs. abandonment)
- **Secret snippet moment** integrated into the crisis (choosing to leave power behind)
- **Convergence path** to caravan (rescued survivors joining westward merchants)
- **Established doubt** creates ongoing internal conflict throughout campaign

The Breaker enters the convergence scene as *the one who chose powerlessness to save others*. This shapes their entry into the larger group differently than heroes who arrive through capability or protection.

---

## Setup Notes (For GM)

**What the GM knows that the player doesn't:**

- The Breaker's arrival in the inn was ordinary (traveling, seeking shelter)
- The earthquake happened without warning
- The hammer is *theirs* but they don't remember acquiring it
- The structural situation is real—pulling the hammer will destabilize everything
- External rescuers are digging toward the collapsed building
- The other trapped people are genuine civilians, not tests or illusions
- Time is passing—the man's trapped leg is getting worse

**Contested-site context (see [[WIZARD_AND_LICH_CADRE]] §Phase 4 and [[STORY_ARC_SYNTHESIS]]):**
The inn's collapse was not the earthquake it appeared to be. The Wizard's agents caused or accelerated it — a structure collapse is an efficient way to neutralize a newly-expelled hero before they can act. The mana-deadening that resulted (see [[CORE_CAMPAIGN_NARRATIVE]]) was an unintended consequence: the magical violence of the agents' intervention burned out the area's magical conductivity, which paradoxically became the Breaker's protection — the deadened zone makes scrying and detection unreliable. The agents created the trap and then lost the ability to confirm it worked. External rescuers digging toward the ruin are ordinary people; the agents, unable to verify the kill, may be watching the rescue from a distance.

**Passive hook:** The mana-deadened zone is the thread. It's distinctive enough that a knowledgeable NPC — a Dwarven archivist, a High Elf elder, or a mage who studies dead zones — can eventually identify it as the signature of violent magical intervention, not a natural collapse. "Something burned the magic out of this place. That doesn't happen by accident." The Breaker need never pursue this; but if they do, the ruin becomes the first physical evidence of the agents' presence in the world.

**Dual-truth preservation:**
- Storyteller knows: The Breaker was placed here — protected by the very damage done to trap them
- Player knows: They woke trapped with a tool that could save them or doom everyone

## Segment 1: The Waking (3-5 minutes)

### Opening Narration

**[Start with pure sensation. No context. No explanation. Player should feel the confinement physically.]**

![[Waking in the Dark - Breaker MUSIC.mp3]]

Darkness, and the weight of stone.

Awareness comes swaddled like a newborn, urgent, raw, and with little understanding. Surrounded by a pressure not quite pain; Not yet. It's the slow compression of massive things seemingly everywhere, no angle neglected.

The air tastes wrong: Chalky and full of dust that coats the tongue, delivering the sour-sweet reek of spilled wine, old smoke, and... what seems like unwashed warmth. Perhaps the echoes of long habitation and sweat.

Breathing takes work. Each inhalation a negotiation with an unforgiving weight that presses but hasn't decided whether to finish its crushing task: Predatory, pressing down to pin while it relishes the moment.

Water drips somewhere. A muted sound that suffuses rather than echoes, directionless, like the darkness weeping.

Your hand flexes and is greeted by wood in return—splintered planks complete the handshake and a warning, one edge smooth where it'd been planed true, the other raw and torn. The timber shifts slightly, and you freeze. Waiting. Listening for the groan of settling mass, and the creak like an admonition that precedes collapse.

Nothing.

Tentative, probing movements reveal the shape of confinement: Something massive pins the left side, not just through weight but also geometry—a limb or makeshift pillar wedged between surfaces that permits no extraction without risking what holds them apart.

On your right side, fractionally more liberty. Exploring that space, your fingers find something surprising: Metal, cold and patient, bold and strong. Tracing up you find its partner: Wood worn smooth. The heft, the craft, the shape all speak of iron shaped for a single purpose.

A hammer.

Not a carpenter's tool—this is cruder, heavier. The kind meant for driving posts deep into resistant earth or breaking stone that won't yield to lesser persuasion. The handle fits the palm with startling familiarity, though no memory surfaces of whose hands wore it smooth. It simply *is*, resting there as though it has always been there, waiting.

But it won't move.

Pulling tests the hammer's position: wedged tight between timber and something harder—stone, maybe, or the compressed ruin of what used to be a wall. Wedged at an angle that suggests it's not just trapped but *load-bearing*, part of whatever geometry keeps this pocket of air from becoming a tomb. The handle points toward a gap—narrow, maybe wide enough for a body to slip through, but not a body *and* this much iron.

The choice crystallizes even before full consciousness returns: leave it, or stay with it.

### GM Notes

- Let sensory details land before moving forward
- Don't rush through the waking sequence
- The disorientation is the point; player should feel genuinely confused
- The tool's presence is *weight* and *identity*, not just utility
- The confinement is specific—predatory, deliberate—it speaks to being tested
- Allow pause after hammer discovery for the player to orient
- The choice (leave it/stay with it) should register as significant even without full context

### Player Agency

The player is now awake and aware of:
- Their own body (confined, pinned on one side)
- The tool (bound to them, wedged, won't move easily)
- The darkness (complete, oppressive)
- The pressure (threatening, patient)

They can act. Prompt them: "What do you do?"

## Segment 2: The Geometry (5-8 minutes)

### Exploration and Understanding

**[Player explores their confinement. Discovery through touch and careful movement.]**

Then breathing interrupts the calculation.

Not personal breathing—someone else's, close but invisible in this absolute black. Shallow, rapid breaths that hitch and catch, the rhythm of fear barely contained. Another sound emerges from farther away: wet coughing, then a child's whimper, quickly stifled.

Multiple someones. Trapped in here with—

But before that realization can settle, exploration begins. Careful exploration—testing each movement, waiting for the groan of shifting weight. Eyes adjust enough to distinguish between absolute darkness and the fractionally lesser black where cracks in the ruin admit ghost-light. Not enough to see, but enough to sense dimension. This space extends perhaps twice shoulder-width, maybe three body-lengths in what used to be—what? A room? A hall?

The air suggests answers: that sour wine smell, the ghost of roasted meat, the particular musk of many bodies sharing space. A public house, maybe. An inn. Somewhere travelers gathered and paid for beds and meals and whatever warmth coin could purchase.

Then the earth moved, and now—this.

The hammer hasn't moved, still wedged in its load-bearing position. But the gap it guards has become clearer through exploration: a space where collapse created a tunnel, narrow but navigable. For a body alone. For someone willing to slip through without the iron weight that won't fit.

Freedom, that way. Air and light and the absence of tons of ruin waiting to settle.

But pulling the hammer free—testing it again confirms the suspicion—would shift the timber it braces. And that timber holds back the floor above. And the floor above holds back the walls. And the walls keep the entire structure from progressive collapse into the spaces where five people breathe and wait and hope for rescue that might already be digging toward them from the outside.

The geometry is merciless: stay with the tool and stay trapped, or leave it wedged and slip through to safety.

Without it. Powerless. Empty-handed.

### GM Notes

- Let player explore at their own pace
- The geometry discovery should feel like puzzle-solving, not exposition
- Air/smell details let them infer "inn" without being told
- Hammer's structural role becomes clear through testing
- Gap discovery offers hope, then immediately complicates
- The breathing sounds at the beginning hint at others, setting up Segment 3
- "Merciless geometry" = the trap crystallizes

### Player Agency

The player now understands:
- The confined space's rough dimensions
- This was likely an inn (inferred from smells)
- The hammer is load-bearing (structural, not just stuck)
- A gap exists that could allow escape
- Body OR hammer can fit through, not both
- Others are present (breathing sounds)
- Pulling hammer = risk collapse

They can:
- Test the gap more thoroughly
- Try communicating toward the breathing sounds
- Attempt to understand the structural situation better
- Ask questions about their situation

Prompt them: "What do you do?"

## Segment 3: The Others (3-5 minutes)

### Discovery of the Trapped

**[Player begins to locate and understand who else is trapped in this darkness.]**

Moving through the compressed space, other presences reveal themselves:

The man who spoke first is trapped in a similar pocket, visible now only as darkness slightly deeper than the surrounding black. His breathing has steadied into something approximating control, but the effort shows. "My leg," he says, when approach brings proximity. "Can't feel it anymore. Pinned."

Beyond him, where the floor has buckled downward, two more: a woman, unconscious, something wet and dark matting her hair where impact found her. Beside her, someone older—gender unclear in this lightless space—who stirs at approach, groaning.

And above, in that sagging pocket of temporary survival: the child. Invisible now, tracked only by sound. The whimpering has stopped, replaced by the careful silence of prey that understands predators hunt by noise.

The child whimpers again, and the sound locates them: somewhere to the right and above, where something hasn't quite collapsed but has sagged down like canvas weighted with snow. A pocket within the pocket, held by timber that won't hold forever.

### GM Notes

- This segment reveals the ethical stakes
- Each trapped person represents a different kind of urgency:
  - Man: conscious, in pain, deteriorating (ticking clock)
  - Woman: unconscious, injured (helpless)
  - Older person: fragile, barely responsive (mortality)
  - Child: afraid, vulnerable (innocence)
- Don't rush the discovery—let each presence register
- The pockets-within-pockets architecture matters: they're separate but connected
- Child's location (above, in sagging section) creates extra tension
- This is where "five people total" becomes real, not abstract

### Player Agency

The player now knows:
- Five people total are trapped (including themselves)
- Each person is in a separate pocket
- Injuries vary: pinned leg, head wound, general trauma
- At least one child is present
- The structure is barely holding
- Some are conscious, some are not

They can:
- Try to communicate more with the conscious ones
- Assess injuries from a distance
- Consider whether anyone can be reached
- Weigh risks of movement

Prompt them: "What do you do?"

## Segment 4: The Voices (3-4 minutes)

### First Contact and Questions

**[Communication begins. Fear, hope, and desperation mix in the darkness.]**

"Is someone there?"

A man's voice, hoarse with dust and terror he's working to master. Trying to map this darkness through sound alone, to make it comprehensible.

"Don't move. The structure isn't stable."

The words surface without thought, carrying a certainty that has no source. Why speak them? Why those words first? No answer comes.

"Are we..." The man's voice wavers. "Is anyone else—"

He doesn't finish. Speaking the possibility might make it real.

"How many?" the man asks.

"Five. At least five."

Including the one doing the counting.

The child begins to cry—soft, defeated sounds that speak of understanding beyond years. The woman stirs but doesn't wake. The man with the pinned leg tries to shift position and gasps, pain breaking through his carefully maintained control. The older person's breathing has gone shallow and irregular, each inhalation a question mark.

And here, in this pocket of temporary survival, hands rest on a tool that could mean everything or nothing, trying to remember if a choice like this has presented itself before.

The answer comes slowly, like light through cracks:

*Yes.*

### GM Notes

- First dialogue establishes humanity in the darkness
- "Don't move" shows protagonist's instinctive understanding of danger
- The man's unfinished question ("Is anyone else—") carries weight of "alive" or "coming"
- "How many?" = first attempt to understand scope of tragedy
- Each trapped person's sounds create urgency without manipulation:
  - Child crying (soft, not hysterical)
  - Woman stirring (hope? or just reflex?)
  - Man gasping (pain worsening)
  - Older person's irregular breathing (time running out)
- The final "Yes" is crucial—first hint of alignment memory
- This sets up Segment 5 (The Choice)

### Player Agency

The player now understands:
- Others are conscious and aware
- Communication is possible but limited
- The man is deteriorating (leg injury worsening)
- Time matters—this won't hold forever
- Something about this feels *familiar* ("Yes" moment)

They can:
- Ask the man questions
- Try to reassure the child
- Assess how long they have
- Consider their options
- Think about what "Yes" means

The crisis is now fully established. Segment 5 will force the choice.

## Segment 5: The Choice (8-12 minutes, Variable)

### The Weight of Decision

**[The crisis is now fully visible. Time for the player to wrestle with what this means.]**

The gap is there. Narrow. Maybe wide enough for shoulders turned sideways, hips scraping stone. Air and light somewhere beyond.

Your body could fit.

The hammer couldn't.

Testing the iron head against the opening: too wide. The handle: too long. The wedged position between timber and stone: load-bearing. Pulling it free would shift the timber. The timber holds the floor. The floor holds the walls. Everything holds everything else.

*"I'm right here."*

The thought arrives without invitation. Not quite yours. Not quite separate.

The man with the pinned leg coughs—wet, rattling. "How long?" Voice thin as the air. "How long until... someone comes?"

No answer for that. Maybe rescuers are digging. Maybe hours. Maybe they've already given up.

The child whimpers from above. The older person's breathing: shallower. Each inhalation a question.

"There's a gap," you say. "Here. Narrow. One person could—"

"Can we all—" The woman stirs, half-conscious.

"No."

Silence weighs more than rubble.

The man understands. "You could get out. Get help."

Could. The word hangs there.

*"You're wasting me."*

The hammer's weight shifts in your grip. Not heavier. Just... present. Insistent.

Your hands know this tool. Don't remember picking it up, but they know it. The worn wood fits like it was shaped by these palms. The balance speaks of use, of purpose, of impacts delivered with certainty.

Options present themselves through touch:

The gap. Body alone. Leave the hammer wedged, slip through to freedom. Get help. Come back.

*If* there's time. *If* the structure holds. *If* rescue arrives before the man's leg fails, before the older person stops breathing, before the child's whimpering becomes something worse.

Or.

Your grip tightens. The angle presents itself: *there*. Where the gap's edge meets timber. One swing. Maybe two. Widen it enough to—

*"I can do this."*

The certainty arrives like muscle memory. You've done this before. Not this exact thing, but... breaking through. Making space where none existed. The hammer knows. Your body knows.

But the timber would shift. The wedged position would fail. Everything above would—

Might. *Might* collapse. Or might hold. Or might settle differently. No way to know until the swing connects.

Or.

The hammer as lever, not weapon. The handle under the timber, another piece of debris as fulcrum. Lift instead of break. Build supports. Wedge. Stabilize. Create rather than destroy.

Slower. Uncertain. You've never done this before—or if you have, the memory doesn't surface the way breaking does. No muscle certainty. No instinctive knowing.

*"Stop thinking. Just swing."*

The man shifts position. Gasps. His breathing has changed—shallower, faster. Pain or shock or both.

The child has gone quiet. That feels worse.

The older person's inhalation stutters, catches, continues.

Your hands rest on the hammer. The wood is warm from your grip. The iron head points toward the gap, toward the angle where one good impact would—

What are you?

Someone who breaks through obstacles. Someone who makes space. Someone whose capability is measured in walls that come down, barriers that shatter, problems that cease to exist under sufficient force.

Or someone who can choose differently. Someone who builds instead of breaks. Someone who takes the slow way, the uncertain way, because fast might mean everyone dies.

The hammer sits patient between timber and stone. Wedged. Load-bearing. Yours.

What are you?

*"Use me. That's what I'm for."*

### GM Notes

**This is the archetype test. Let it breathe.**

- Don't rush the player through this decision
- Three clear options are presented in the prose: escape alone, break wider, build supports
- The hammer's voice creates pressure but not certainty
- The other trapped people create urgency, but this isn't about guilt-tripping
- Player may explore other creative solutions—allow it, but the geometry is unforgiving
- Player may ask questions—answer honestly about structural reality
- No option is guaranteed to succeed—that's the crisis

**The three paths laid out in prose:**

1. **Slip through the gap alone** (leave hammer wedged)
   - Fastest route to getting help
   - Requires abandoning the tool
   - Risk: takes time to return, others may not survive the wait
   - Identity: "I can serve without my capability"

2. **Break the gap wider** (swing hammer, force the space)
   - Feels instinctive, certain, familiar
   - Hammer knows this, body knows this
   - Risk: might trigger total collapse, kill everyone
   - Identity: "I am a breaker. This is what I do."

3. **Build supports** (use hammer as lever, wedge debris, stabilize)
   - Slower, uncertain, unfamiliar
   - No muscle memory for this
   - Risk: takes time, might fail, might not work at all
   - Identity: "I can choose to create instead of destroy"

**Key questions player might ask:**

*"How confident am I that breaking would work?"*
- The body feels certain. The hammer feels eager. But certainty isn't guarantee. You've broken through worse—but not with five lives at stake.

*"How long would building take?"*
- Unknown. Minutes? An hour? The man's breathing suggests you don't have hours.

*"Could I slip through and come back quickly?"*
- Maybe. Depends on how close rescuers are. Depends if the structure holds that long.

*"What if I try something else?"*
- Allow creative solutions. But the physics are unforgiving. The hammer is wedged load-bearing. The gap is narrow. The pockets are separate.

**Two snippet paths based on thematic choice:**

**Nature Path** (break wider, slip through alone, trust capability):
- Snippet focuses on: destruction as tool, capability as identity, trusting your nature
- Memory: a time when breaking through saved lives

**Duty Path** (build supports, help others first, restrain power):
- Snippet focuses on: restraint as strength, service over self, choosing against instinct
- Memory: a time when refusing to break saved lives

**The "Yes" moment from Segment 4 should haunt this choice:**
- You've faced this before: trust your nature or choose restraint
- Whatever path they choose, the memory will surface
- The decision triggers full emergence of the snippet

### Player Agency

The player must decide their approach:
- **Slip through alone** - Leave hammer, get help, return
- **Break it wider** - Trust instinct, swing hammer, force the gap
- **Build supports** - Use hammer as lever, create stability, slow path
- **Other creative solution** - GM adjudicates feasibility within structural constraints

Prompt them: "What do you do?"

**Do not proceed to Segment 6 until the player has made their choice and begun acting on it.**
