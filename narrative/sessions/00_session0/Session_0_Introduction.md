## The Pitch

*Read this section aloud to your players.*

A thousand years ago, an empire fell. The peoples it had subjugated — Orcs, Dwarves, Elves, Humans, and others — rebuilt their world along the trade roads that had always connected them, long before any conqueror drew borders. Now those roads are thriving again: merchant caravans thread through mountain passes and desert basins, carrying silk, spices, stories, and secrets between cities that have learned to define themselves on their own terms.

But something is wrong. It lives in the silence between market calls, in the way old scholars pause mid-sentence and stare east, in the fact that certain ruins are avoided not because they are dangerous but because they feel *attentive*. The world is rich, alive, and beautiful — and underneath all of it, something unfinished is stirring. You don't know what it is yet. You only know that you arrive in a world that calls you to answer.

---

## Tone & Feel

**Mythic, Atmospheric, Morally Complex, Intimate, Epic, Melancholic, Hopeful**

This is a campaign of sensory immersion and slow revelation. Expect long stretches of human-scale drama — trust earned and tested, debts owed and repaid, strangers becoming companions — punctuated by moments of mythic intensity that reframe everything that came before. The tone shifts between the warmth of a fire shared with strangers and the vertigo of realizing the ground beneath you is not as solid as you believed. Comedy lives here too, but it is the comedy of exhaustion, resilience, and the absurdity of people who keep walking when every reasonable instinct says to stop.

---

## Themes

Each theme below is also a question the campaign will ask — of the characters, and of you as players.

**Duty vs. Understanding** — Is it enough to do the right thing, or must you also understand *why* it's right? What happens when you complete a task without grasping its full consequences?

**Identity Through Method** — You are not just *what* you do, but *how* you do it. When your method is the thing you doubt most, who are you?

**Trust Across Difference** — The Silk Road exists because people who share nothing in common still choose to trade, to talk, to travel together. What does it cost to extend trust to someone whose world looks nothing like yours?

**The Weight of the Past** — History is not a story told by the victors. It is a living thing carried by everyone who survived it — in their languages, their grudges, their trade routes, and the names they refuse to change.

**The Unknown Summons** — The world is not broken. It is *incomplete*. Something recognizes that incompleteness. The difference between those two things matters enormously.

## Overview

*Give your players the following information before character creation.*

The world of Tarim Shaiel stretches along the great trade arteries that once connected an empire — and which now, a millennium after that empire's collapse, have reasserted themselves as the natural channels of commerce, culture, and connection between free peoples.

To the east lies Chang'an, the old imperial capital: still vast, still grand, but fading. Its prestige rests on memories of administrative power that no longer flows through its halls. Westward, the great cities of Samarkand, Bukhara, and Tashkent have shed their garrison identities and become genuine crossroads — cosmopolitan, ambitious, and increasingly shaped by Orcish merchant influence. Between them, mountain passes guarded by Dwarven trade-strongholds control access with a merchant's precision: you may enter, but only on terms that serve the Vault. In the eastern borderlands, Elven sacred sites predate the empire entirely, their cave-temple complexes drawing pilgrims along routes that have been walked for millennia. And everywhere, along every road, the caravanserais — those way-stations of rest, trade, and information — serve as the connective tissue of a world that has learned to organize itself around movement rather than control.

Magic exists here, but it is not tidy. It flows through channels that scholars have mapped imperfectly and that practitioners access through tradition, intuition, and risk. Some magic is structured and studied; some is wild, geographic, tied to places that predate any civilization's memory. The peoples of this world have their own relationships with these forces — the Elves through ancient contemplative practice, the Orcs through resonance with the land they have reclaimed, the Dwarves through craft that blurs the line between engineering and enchantment.

The Empire that once controlled these lands is gone. Its fall was not clean. The seventy years that followed were marked by chaos, displacement, and the slow, painful work of peoples learning to govern themselves after generations of subjugation. That work is largely done — but its scars remain in ruined cities, in cultural memories, in the way certain peoples still regard each other with inherited wariness. The Orcs, in particular, carry the longest memory of what imperial "order" actually meant.

What the players need to know is this: the world is alive, functioning, and in many ways thriving. Trade flows. Cities grow. People build lives. And yet — there is something underneath it all that does not sit right. A wrongness that defies easy naming. Old scholars feel it in their research. Travelers sense it in the quality of the silence at certain crossroads. It is not a crisis. Not yet. It is the space where a crisis will eventually live, and some part of you — for reasons you cannot yet articulate — knows that you are meant to understand it.

Long enough for the mountain passes to be held by people who understand that a door is most valuable when you control who walks through it.    And the roads are busy now.

   Listen: you can hear it if you stop moving long enough. The creak of wooden axles. The murmur of a dozen languages braided into a single negotiation. Further still, a woman is selling silk that took three months to reach her stall, and she is arguing about the price with the same ferocity her thrice-great grandmother argued about the price of grain under occupation. Some things survive every catastrophe.
>

   There is a silence, too. Not the absence of sound — the roads are never truly quiet. This is something else. A held breath. A pause in the music that no one can quite identify because the song keeps playing. Something--amiss.
 >
   Scholars notice it first, because scholars are trained to hear the gaps between what is said. Travelers notice it next, because travelers learn to read the quality of a road the way a sailor reads water — and something in the quality has changed. Not broken. Not dangerous. Just... expectant.
>
   As if the road itself is waiting for someone.
>

## Player Principles

*Keep the following in mind as you play.*

**Trust Your Instincts, Then Question Them** — Your character may feel things they cannot explain: recognition without memory, certainty without evidence, fear without cause. Follow your instincts. They are real. But do not assume they are complete.

**Earn Your Relationships** — You will begin as strangers. Whatever bonds form between your characters must be built through shared experience, not assumed by the premise. The campaign may give you reasons to trust each other. It may also give you reasons not to.

**Let Yourself Not Know** — This is a campaign of slow revelation. You will have questions that are not answered for sessions. Resist the urge to solve the mystery before the mystery is ready to be solved. Sit with uncertainty. Your character is sitting with it too.

**Your Method Is Your Identity** — How your character approaches problems could be as important than what problems they solve. A character who embraces restraint is telling a different story than a character who prevails through dominance. Try to pay attention to *how* you act, not just *what* you accomplish.

**The World Was Here Before You** — You are entering a living world with a thousand years of post-imperial history. The peoples you meet have their own concerns, their own memories, their own reasons for the choices they make. Enjoy engaging with them as full participants in the story, not as set dressing for yours.

**You Guide The Ship** — Your characters are here a vehicles for your creativity, and so is the WHOLE world. There will be moments your story carves shape into events of the world, and that is AWESOME. Be willing to step-in, when invited, to add to the lore; Invent, engage, and grow the story. Your efforts not only make everyone's experience richer, it helps shape the story you all will experience. Be storytellers!
### Above all that..

→ Be a fan of your character and their journey. 
→ Spotlight your friends. 
→ Address the characters and address the players. 
→ Build the world together. 
→ Play to find out what happens. 
→ Hold on gently.

## GM Principles

*Keep the following guidance in mind while you run this campaign.*

**Lead With Sensation, Not Exposition** — When you describe a place, start with what the characters smell, hear, and feel underfoot. Let context emerge through detail, not summary. A refugee camp is not "about thirty people hiding in a ravine." It is the smell of fear-sweat, the sound of a child who has learned not to cry too loud, and the mud that has given up being soil.

**Make Every Culture a Full Participant** — The Orcs are not "the warrior race." The Dwarves are not "the crafting race." Every people in this world has a complex relationship with the Empire's legacy, with trade, with magic, and with each other. When in doubt, ask yourself: what does this community want on its own terms?

**Weaponize Silence** — The most powerful moments in this campaign will not be combat encounters or dramatic reveals. They will be the pauses. The held eye contact. The moment when a character realizes they recognize something they shouldn't. Give those moments room to breathe. Do not fill them with narration.

**Doubt Is the Real Antagonist** — External threats exist and they are real. But the force that will most effectively challenge these characters is their own uncertainty about who they are and whether their methods are worthy. Nurture that doubt. Do not resolve it for them.

**Reward Restraint** — In this campaign, choosing *not* to act is often more heroic than acting. Build encounters where the obvious solution — the one that uses the character's greatest strength at full power — is available but carries costs. Make restraint feel like a meaningful, powerful choice rather than a failure to engage.

**Protect the Mystery** — You know things the players do not. Guard that knowledge carefully. When players ask questions you cannot yet answer, do not deflect awkwardly — acknowledge the question with gravity. "That's a very good question" is a complete sentence. The mystery is an asset. Spend it wisely.

## Safety & Content

This campaign engages with morally complex themes. These tools keep the table a space where difficult material creates meaning, not distress.

**Lines & Veils** — Establish at Session Zero what content should never appear (lines) and what should happen off-screen (veils).

**X-Card** — Any player can tap the X-Card (or type "X") at any time to skip, rewind, or edit a scene — no explanation needed.

**Open Door** — Any player may step away from the table at any time without comment or consequence.

**Check-Ins** — Brief check-in at session start and end to surface anything that needs adjustment.

**Content to discuss and calibrate at Session Zero:**

Grief & Loss · Moral Ambiguity · Consequences of Violence · Liberation & Its Aftermath · Identity Crisis · Cosmic/Existential Dread · Self-Doubt · Complicity & Silence · Generational Consequence

## Archetypes

Players choose an archetype — not a mechanical class, but a thematic role describing *how* your hero changed the world. Each carries a question the campaign will not stop asking. No two characters should share an archetype; different methods in friction create the campaign's best moments.

**The Warrior** — *"Was force the right answer — or did it prevent me from seeing what I should have seen?"*

**The Seeker** — *"Did the truths I found serve the people I found them for — or did they find other uses?"*

**The Breaker** — *"Is breaking things my greatest gift — or the only thing I know how to do?"*

**The Visionary** — *"Was I seeing truth — or seeing what I was meant to see?"*

**The Bridge** — *"Is the unity I built genuine — or did I find ways to make people stop fighting long enough to serve a purpose?"*

**The Diplomat** — *"Was my willingness to work within systems wisdom — or did I leave standing what should have been torn down?"*

**The Sacrificer** — *"Is my willingness to give my greatest strength — or a vulnerability that others have always known how to use?"*

**The Martyr** — *"Was the sacrifice that defined us genuine — or was it arranged, and does the difference matter now?"*

**The Sentinel** — *"At what point does the faithful observer become the one who could have spoken — and chose not to?"*

**The Keeper** — *"Was I protecting what was worth keeping — or holding alive what should have been allowed to end?"*

**The Trickster** — *"Can you wear enough masks without losing what was underneath — and do I still know what that was?"*

*Full archetype descriptions — including Vestiges and extended mechanical guidance — are provided separately.*

## Session Zero Questions

*Ask any of these questions to your players, or create your own. These are designed to situate characters in the world AND calibrate the table's expectations for tone and content.*

### World-Building Questions (Collaborative)

**What is the most valuable thing that travels the trade roads — and it isn't gold or silk?** *(This establishes what your table values: information? stories? trust? people?)*

**Name a place along the road that travelers avoid — not because it's dangerous, but because it feels wrong. What do they say about it?** *(This seeds the "something unfinished" theme and gives players ownership of the world's unease.)*

**What did the Empire take from your character's people that hasn't been fully recovered, even after a thousand years?** *(This grounds the post-imperial world in personal stakes and invites cultural specificity.)*

**Your character carries something they cannot explain — an object, a habit, a recurring dream. What is it?** *(This creates hooks for the GM to connect to the awakening and tool discovery narratives without revealing the mechanism.)*

### Tone & Content Calibration

**This campaign is poised to explore self-doubt, moral ambiguity, and characters questioning whether their greatest strengths might also be their greatest flaws. How does this table want to engage with those themes?** *(This opens a conversation about emotional intensity, vulnerability, and how much internal conflict players want.)*

**Moments of intense restraint — choosing not to use power, choosing silence over action — can be as important as moments of decisive force. Is that something this table is excited about?** *(This sets expectations for the campaign's rhythm and prevents frustration when "the obvious move" isn't the right one.)*

**Characters in this campaign may receive private information that other players don't have. How does this table feel about secrets between characters?** *(This calibrates for the secret snippet system and private flashbacks.)*

**How does this table want to handle scenes of displacement, refugees, and the aftermath of empire? These are present in the world and will be encountered. What level of specificity feels right?** *(This is a safety and sensitivity calibration — the Silk Road setting involves real historical parallels that deserve intentional handling.)*

## Session Flow Overview

*Share this with your players so they know what to expect from the structure of the session.*

Session 0 is divided into three acts. The session will run approximately 4-5 hours.

**Act 1: Individual Introductions ("Awakenings").** Each player will have a solo scenario lasting approximately 40-45 minutes. While one player is in their scenario, the others will be spectators — watching, learning, and forming first impressions of characters they haven't yet met. Each introduction places your character alone in an unfamiliar situation that tests who they are at their core. There are no wrong answers. There is no combat in the traditional sense. What matters is how you respond.

*[GM Note: Do not reveal that all awakenings converge. Let the players discover the connection organically.]*

**Act 2: Convergence.** Through the natural resolution of your individual scenarios, your characters will find their paths intersecting. You will meet as strangers. You will have reasons to travel together — debts, obligations, coincidence, necessity. Whether those reasons become something more is up to you.

**Act 3: The First Night.** The session ends around a campfire. What happens there will not be explained in advance.

*[GM Note: The recognition climax is an imposed GM narration. Do not preview it. Do not hint at it. Let it land.]*
