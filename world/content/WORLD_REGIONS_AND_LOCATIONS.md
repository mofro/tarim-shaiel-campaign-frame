# Tarim-Shaiel: World Regions & Locations

## Overview

The Tarim-Shaiel world (Silk Road-inspired) is organized into **6 major regions**, each with distinct geographic, political, and cultural characteristics shaped by the Empire's collapse and post-liberation stabilization.

Each region contains a curated set of locations. Locations are bucketed by **cultural influence** (which language/era their names reflect) and **era named** (when the current name was adopted).

---

## Regional Structure

### 1. EASTERN TERMINUS
**Chang'an Region**

**Geography:** Imperial China heartland; river valley, agricultural core
**Historical Role:** Supreme control seat of the Empire; seat of imperial power
**Post-Liberation Status:** Reduced prestige; struggling for relevance; overshadowed by vibrant trade-based economies
**Cultural Dominance:** Human (Imperial legacy)
**Naming Convention:** Empire-era names retained; rarely changing

**Locations (1):**

| Name | Type | Cultural Influence | Era Named | Notes |
|------|------|-------------------|-----------|-------|
| Chang'an | city | Human/Imperial | Empire | Supreme control seat; imperial court; old prestige fading |

**Region Narrative:**
Chang'an was the beating heart of the Empire. Its size, walls, and administrative infrastructure were unmatched. Post-liberation, it remains large and prestigious, but its economic relevance declined sharply. The empire's fall meant less imperial procurement, less military spending, less bureaucratic density. Merchants prefer hubs closer to actual trade flows. The city struggles to maintain relevance while the merchant-driven economy booms elsewhere.

### 2. CENTRAL ASIAN HUBS
**Transoxiana & Western Basin**

**Geography:** Western Central Asia; oasis cities and valleys; major crossroads
**Historical Role:** Control seats, garrison hubs, resource extraction centers; Empire imposed heavy administrative/military presence
**Post-Liberation Status:** Thriving; rebuilt as genuine trade capitals; increasingly cosmopolitan with emerging Orc influence
**Cultural Dominance:** Human (Empire names retained) + Orcish (emerging influence)
**Naming Convention:** Empire-era names retained officially, but with post-stabilization Orc/merchant cultural undercurrents

**Locations (5):**

| Name | Type | Cultural Influence | Era Named | Historical Names | Notes |
|------|------|-------------------|-----------|------------------|-------|
| Samarkand | city | Human/Imperial | Empire | — | Major control seat; maintained imperial order; still prestigious |
| Bukhara | city | Human/Imperial | Empire | — | Garrison hub; scholarly center; Empire infrastructure persists |
| Tashkent | city | Human/Imperial | Empire | — | Central Asian hub; largest city; mixed populations emerging |
| Balkh | city | Human/Imperial (ruins) | Empire | — | Once "Mother of Cities"; destroyed during chaos; now ruins with political significance |
| Merv | city | Human/Imperial (rebuilt) | Empire | Mary (post-liberation name, rarely used) | Destroyed then rebuilt; shadow of former glory; Orc influence growing |

**Region Narrative:**
These were the Empire's showcase cities—heavily garrisoned, administratively dense, culturally imposed with imperial aesthetics. They were designed for control, not genuine trade. Yet trade happened anyway (people trade regardless of politics). Post-liberation, these cities faced an identity crisis: the Empire's apparatus vanished, but the cities remained functional. Over generations, they evolved toward genuine trade hubs, attracting Orc merchants, diverse populations, and merchant guilds. They retain Empire-era names (locals never bothered to change them officially), but the culture underneath is increasingly cosmopolitan and Orc-influenced.

### 3. EASTERN GATEWAY
**Gansu Corridor & Sacred Borderlands**

**Geography:** Gansu Corridor; transition zone between empire and Tarim; border/gateway zone
**Historical Role:** Border control; imperial frontier enforcement; gatekeeping into empire
**Post-Liberation Status:** Elven sacred sites persist; human imperial structures fading; sacred pilgrimages continue
**Cultural Dominance:** Elven (ancient, sacred) + Human (imperial fortifications)
**Naming Convention:** Elven sacred names (ancient) + Human imperial names (recent, fading)

**Locations (2):**

| Name | Type | Cultural Influence | Era Named | Sacred Significance | Notes |
|------|------|-------------------|-----------|---------------------|-------|
| Dunhuang | sacred-site | Elven/Sacred | Ancient (Elven) | Mogao Caves; 366+ Buddhist caves with millennia of art; major pilgrimage | Predates Empire; sacred to Elven traditions; Buddhist overlay adds depth |
| Jade Gate | route-node | Human/Imperial | Empire | Imperial checkpoint; border enforcement | Empire fortification; still functions as checkpoint but fading relevance |

**Region Narrative:**
Dunhuang is sacred—far older than the Empire, rooted in Elven spiritual traditions and Buddhist pilgrimages. The Mogao Caves contain centuries of art and religious teaching. The Empire left it alone (wisely), recognizing that disrupting pilgrimage sites creates unnecessary resistance. Jade Gate was the Empire's attempt to control the boundary; it still functions as a checkpoint, but with the Empire gone, its authority is more ritual than enforcement. Elves remain withdrawn and separate; humans, Goblins, and pilgrims pass through.

### 4. TARIM BASIN OASES
**Central Silk Road Network**

**Geography:** Tarim Basin; oasis network surrounding Taklamakan Desert; central trade crossroads
**Historical Role:** Resource extraction (salt, jade, rare goods); trade flow control (ineffective—too large to control comprehensively)
**Post-Liberation Status:** Booming; genuinely cosmopolitan; trade thrives because it was always the natural flow
**Cultural Dominance:** Mixed (Elven sacred sites + Orc traders + Human merchants + Goblin diaspora + Dwarven influence in edges)
**Naming Convention:** Varied; some Elven sacred names (ancient), some Orc names (chaos/stabilization era), some cosmopolitan hybrid names

**Region Narrative:**
The Tarim Basin oases are the *true* heart of the Silk Road. Trade flows here naturally because the geography demands it—merchants need water, rest, and supplies. These locations barely noticed the Empire's collapse because trade was never about imperial permission; it was about necessity and mutual benefit. Post-liberation, this region exploded with cosmopolitan energy. Orc traders, human merchants, Goblin information brokers, Elven pilgrims, and Dwarven craftspeople all converge here. Some locations retain Elven sacred names (Dunhuang, Kucha). Others adopted names during the chaos era (Orcish influences). Most use modern cosmopolitan names that blend influences. This is where the real power of the new world lies—not in imperial control seats, but in genuine economic interdependence.

### 5. MOUNTAIN PASSES & GATEWAYS
**Dwarven Strongholds**

**Geography:** Hindu Kush, Pamir mountains; mountain passages; natural gateways between regions
**Historical Role:** Empire taxed by Dwarves (never truly controlled); Dwarves maintained independence despite Empire pressure
**Post-Liberation Status:** Independent; gatekeepers with mutual interest in trade flow; stable, respected
**Cultural Dominance:** Dwarvish (ALWAYS maintained own names; Empire never succeeded in renaming)
**Naming Convention:** Dwarvish names (Slavic-influenced compounds meaning the actual geographic feature or strategic function)

**Locations (3):**

| Name | Type | Cultural Influence | Era Named | Historical Context | Notes |
|------|------|-------------------|-----------|-------------------|-------|
| Maralbashi | route-node | Dwarvish | Ancient (Dwarven) | Empire attempted taxation; Dwarves refused renaming | Gateway to western passes; guides and porters; final resupply before high altitude |
| Aksu | route-node | Dwarvish | Ancient (Dwarven) | Empire attempted control; Dwarves maintained autonomy | Waypoint on northern corridor; established merchant infrastructure |
| Shorchuk | route-node | Dwarvish | Ancient (Dwarven) | Empire recognized Dwarven authority | Mountain passage waypoint; transition between oasis and high altitude |

**Region Narrative:**
Dwarves control these passes and they always have. Even during the Empire's height, Dwarves refused to accept external naming or control. They collected their own tolls, maintained their own standards, and enforced their own rules. The Empire learned (eventually) not to fight this—Dwarves were too entrenched, too valuable, and too dangerous. Post-liberation, nothing changed structurally because nothing *needed* to change. Dwarves remain independent gatekeepers, but now their role is clearly valuable rather than grudgingly tolerated. Trade flows through their passes, they maintain them, they collect tolls, and everyone respects them for it. These strongholds are connected to the mysterious Far North Dwarven Kingdoms, but details remain secret.

### 6. STEPPE CONFEDERATIONS
**Orcish Nomadic Territories** (Archetype; No Current Locations)

**Geography:** Northern plains; nomadic routes; open grasslands
**Historical Role:** Enslaved labor force; conscripted armies; "controlled" through fear and binding spells
**Post-Liberation Status:** Recently stabilized; Orc confederations now traders and independent powers; still building trust
**Cultural Dominance:** Orcish (chaos-era names replaced with stabilization-era names; old names considered derisive/shameful)
**Naming Convention:** Orcish names (Tolkien guttural style); newer names reflect trade/stability orientation, not chaos/raid orientation

**Locations:** [To be added as campaign develops]

**Region Narrative:**
The Steppe was never truly "controlled" by the Empire despite its attempts. Orcs were enslaved, conscripted, and bound by magic, but they were never actually loyal. The liberation of enslaved Orcs (~1250s) triggered the Chaos Period (~70 years of raiding, territorial conflict, bloodshed). Gradually, through brutal conflict and diplomatic pressure from southern powers, Orc confederations stabilized. They discovered that trade was more profitable than raiding. Over generations, they transitioned from raiders to merchants. Old names from the chaos era (names of raiding bands, warrior tribes) are now embarrassing relics. New names reflect the stabilized, trade-focused confederations. This integration is recent—still within living memory for long-lived races. Trust is fragile but growing.
